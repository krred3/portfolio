Design Optimization of a Nano-Aircraft Wing

Bio-Inspired Aerodynamics for Micro Air Vehicles (MAV)

View Project Report

Micro Air Vehicles (MAVs) operate in a low Reynolds number regime where conventional aerodynamic principles break down and air viscosity dominates. The goal of this research was to design a high-efficiency wing for a dragonfly-inspired MAV by leveraging the "trapped vortex" effect, a phenomenon where corrugated surfaces keep airflow attached better than smooth airfoils at micro-scales. This work is currently being finalized for submission to the AIAA Journal as a peer-reviewed technical paper.

My contribution was transforming the initial 2D theoretical airfoil concepts into fully realized 3D wing geometries. I utilized Ansys Fluent to simulate the complex flow separation and vortex shedding behavior across various corrugation angles. The CFD analysis revealed that specific corrugation patterns could effectively trap vortices, delaying stall and significantly boosting lift. I then translated these aerodynamic profiles into 3D CAD models using Fusion 360, optimizing the planform shape for structural rigidity and printability.

The final optimized wing design achieved a Lift-to-Drag (L/D) ratio of approximately 15, a highly efficient benchmark for nano-scale flight. To validate the simulation, we manufactured physical prototypes using 3D printing and conducted wind tunnel testing. The results confirmed that the bio-mimetic corrugated design outperformed traditional smooth airfoils in this flight regime, validating the transition from theoretical 2D analysis to a functional 3D aerodynamic surface.
